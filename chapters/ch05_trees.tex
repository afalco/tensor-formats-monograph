\chapter{Dimension Partition Trees}
\label{chap:trees}

The Tucker format developed in Chapters~\ref{chap:algtensor}
and~\ref{chap:minsubspaces} corresponds to the shallowest possible
hierarchical structure: all $d$ factor spaces are placed at the same
level below the root. Richer formats arise by organising the dimensions
into a \emph{hierarchy}, encoded by a rooted tree. This chapter
introduces dimension partition trees, establishes their basic properties,
and presents the principal examples (Tucker, Hierarchical Tucker, Tensor
Train). The material prepares the ground for the algebraic
(Chapter~\ref{chap:algTBT}) and topological (Chapter~\ref{chap:topTBT})
development of tree-based tensor formats.

Throughout, $D = \{1, \ldots, d\}$ with $d \geq 2$.

%------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Definition and basic properties}
\label{sec:trees:def}
%------------------------------------------------------------

\begin{definition}[Dimension partition tree]
\label{def:partition_tree}
A finite rooted tree $\TD$ is called a \emph{dimension partition tree
of $D$} if:
\begin{enumerate}
\item[\textup{(a)}] Every vertex $\alpha \in \TD$ is a non-empty
    subset of $D$.
\item[\textup{(b)}] The root of $\TD$ is $D$ itself.
\item[\textup{(c)}] Every non-leaf vertex $\alpha \in \TD$
    (i.e., every $\alpha$ with $\card{\alpha} \geq 2$) has at
    least two children, and the set of children of $\alpha$,
    denoted $\Sons{\alpha}$, forms a \emph{non-trivial partition}
    of $\alpha$ (i.e., a partition into at least two non-empty
    subsets whose union is $\alpha$).
\item[\textup{(d)}] Every vertex $\alpha$ with $\card{\alpha} = 1$
    has no children; such vertices are called \emph{leaves}.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

The set of all leaves of $\TD$ is denoted $\Leaves$.
Condition~(c) forces every non-leaf to have at least two children,
so the tree is never a path of singletons. The following observation
is immediate from the definition.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:leaves_trivial}
For any dimension partition tree $\TD$, the set of leaves coincides
with the set of singletons of $D$:
\begin{equation*}
    \Leaves = \bigl\{\{j\} : j \in D\bigr\}.
\end{equation*}
In particular, $\Leaves$ is the trivial partition of $D$ into
singletons.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By~(d), leaves are exactly the vertices with $\card{\alpha} = 1$,
i.e., the singletons $\{j\}$ for $j \in D$. Since the root is $D$
and each non-leaf splits into a partition of its label set, every
$j \in D$ is eventually reached as a leaf by iterating the partition.
\end{proof}

\subsection*{Levels, depth, and level-partitions}

\begin{definition}
\label{def:level_depth}
The \emph{level} of a vertex $\alpha \in \TD$ is defined recursively by
\begin{equation*}
    \lev{D} = 0,
    \qquad
    \lev{\beta} = \lev{\alpha} + 1
    \quad \text{for } \beta \in \Sons{\alpha}.
\end{equation*}
The \emph{depth} of the tree is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:depth}
    \dep{\TD} \coloneqq \max_{\alpha \in \TD} \lev{\alpha}.
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

Since leaves are the only vertices with $\card{\alpha} = 1$, and
every path from root to leaf passes through a strictly decreasing
chain of subset sizes, the depth satisfies $1 \leq \dep{\TD} \leq d-1$.

\begin{definition}
\label{def:level_partition}
For $1 \leq k \leq \dep{\TD}$, the \emph{$k$-th level partition} of
$D$ induced by $\TD$ is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:level_partition}
    \PkT{k}{\TD}
    \coloneqq \bigl\{\alpha \in \TD : \lev{\alpha} = k\bigr\}.
\end{equation}
By convention, $\PkT{0}{\TD} = \{D\}$.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:level_partition_is_partition}
For each $1 \leq k \leq \dep{\TD}$, the collection
$\PkT{k}{\TD}$ is a partition of $D$. Moreover:
\begin{enumerate}
\item[\textup{(i)}] Every element of $\PkT{k}{\TD}$ is a subset of
    some element of $\PkT{k-1}{\TD}$ \textup{(}refinement property\textup{)}.
\item[\textup{(ii)}] $\PkT{\dep{\TD}}{\TD} = \Leaves$, the trivial
    partition of $D$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The elements of $\PkT{k}{\TD}$ are precisely the vertex labels at
level $k$. Since each such vertex $\alpha$ is the child of a unique
parent $\beta \in \PkT{k-1}{\TD}$ satisfying $\alpha \subset \beta$,
and since $\Sons{\beta}$ partitions $\beta$, the elements of
$\PkT{k}{\TD}$ at level $k$ partition $D$ by induction on $k$. Parts
(i) and (ii) follow directly.
\end{proof}

The sequence of partitions
$\PkT{0}{\TD} \succ \PkT{1}{\TD} \succ \cdots \succ
\PkT{\dep{\TD}}{\TD} = \Leaves$
(where $\mathcal{P} \succ \mathcal{Q}$ means $\mathcal{Q}$ refines
$\mathcal{P}$) captures the hierarchical structure of the tree.
This is the central object connecting dimension partition trees to
tensor spaces: for each level $k$, the identification
$\VD \cong \bigotimes_{\alpha \in \PkT{k}{\TD}} \Vbigalpha$
(Equation~\eqref{eq:iterated_tensor}) allows one to view $\bv \in \VD$
as a Tucker tensor at level $k$.

\section{Special trees and standard formats}
\label{sec:trees:special}
%------------------------------------------------------------

The following three families of dimension partition trees correspond
to the three most widely used tensor formats.

\subsection*{The Tucker tree}

\begin{example}[Tucker tree]
\label{ex:tucker_tree}
The \emph{Tucker tree} $\TDtuck$ over $D$ has depth~1 and satisfies
\begin{equation*}
    \TDtuck = \bigl\{D,\, \{1\},\, \{2\},\, \ldots,\, \{d\}\bigr\},
    \qquad
    \Sons{D} = \Leaves[\TDtuck] = \bigl\{\{j\} : j \in D\bigr\}.
\end{equation*}
The only non-trivial partition step is $D \to \{\{1\},\ldots,\{d\}\}$.
\end{example}

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\synttree[$D$[$\{1\}$][$\{2\}$][$\{3\}$][$\{4\}$][$\{5\}$][$\{6\}$]]
\caption{Tucker tree over $D=\{1,2,3,4,5,6\}$: depth~$1$,
one level partition $\mathcal{P}_1 = \Leaves$.}
\label{fig:tucker_tree}
\end{figure}

The Tucker tree is the unique tree of depth~1 over $D$. The corresponding
tensor format is exactly the Tucker format of
Chapter~\ref{chap:algtensor}:
$\FT{\fr}{\VD}{\TDtuck} = \Tucker{\fr}{\VD}$.

\subsection*{Binary trees and the Hierarchical Tucker format}

\begin{definition}
\label{def:binary_tree}
A dimension partition tree $\TD$ is \emph{binary} if every non-leaf
vertex has exactly two children:
$\card{\Sons{\alpha}} = 2$ for all $\alpha \in \TD$ with
$\card{\alpha} \geq 2$.
\end{definition}

Binary trees are the natural hierarchical structure for the
\emph{Hierarchical Tucker} (HT) format introduced by
Hackbusch and Kühn~\cite{HaKuehn2009}.

\begin{example}[Hierarchical Tucker tree]
\label{ex:HT_tree}
For $D = \{1,2,3,4\}$, the balanced binary HT tree is
\begin{equation*}
    \TDht = \bigl\{D,\,\{1,2\},\,\{3,4\},\,\{1\},\,\{2\},\,\{3\},\,\{4\}\bigr\},
\end{equation*}
with $\Sons{D} = \{\{1,2\},\{3,4\}\}$,
$\Sons{\{1,2\}} = \{\{1\},\{2\}\}$,
$\Sons{\{3,4\}} = \{\{3\},\{4\}\}$.
Here $\dep{\TDht} = 2$, with level partitions
$\mathcal{P}_1 = \{\{1,2\},\{3,4\}\}$ and
$\mathcal{P}_2 = \Leaves = \{\{1\},\{2\},\{3\},\{4\}\}$.
\end{example}

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\synttree[$\{1,2,3,4\}$[$\{1,2\}$[$\{1\}$][$\{2\}$]][$\{3,4\}$[$\{3\}$][$\{4\}$]]]
\caption{Balanced binary tree over $D=\{1,2,3,4\}$: depth~$2$,
corresponding to the HT format.}
\label{fig:HT_tree}
\end{figure}

\begin{example}[Non-binary tree]
\label{ex:nonbinary_tree}
For $D = \{1,2,3,4,5,6\}$:
\begin{equation*}
    \TD = \bigl\{D,\,\{1,2,3\},\,\{4,5\},\,\{6\},\,\{1\},\,\{2,3\},\,
    \{4\},\,\{5\},\,\{2\},\,\{3\}\bigr\},
\end{equation*}
where $\Sons{D} = \{\{1,2,3\},\{4,5\},\{6\}\}$ (three children),
$\Sons{\{1,2,3\}} = \{\{1\},\{2,3\}\}$,
$\Sons{\{2,3\}} = \{\{2\},\{3\}\}$,
$\Sons{\{4,5\}} = \{\{4\},\{5\}\}$.
Here $\dep{\TD} = 3$, with
$\mathcal{P}_1 = \{\{1,2,3\},\{4,5\},\{6\}\}$,
$\mathcal{P}_2 = \{\{1\},\{2,3\},\{4\},\{5\},\{6\}\}$,
$\mathcal{P}_3 = \Leaves$.
\end{example}

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\synttree[$\{1,2,3,4,5,6\}$
  [$\{1,2,3\}$[$\{1\}$][$\{2,3\}$[$\{2\}$][$\{3\}$]]]
  [$\{4,5\}$[$\{4\}$][$\{5\}$]]
  [$\{6\}$]]
\caption{Non-binary dimension partition tree over $D=\{1,2,3,4,5,6\}$:
depth~$3$.}
\label{fig:nonbinary_tree}
\end{figure}

\subsection*{The Tensor Train tree}

\begin{example}[Tensor Train tree]
\label{ex:TT_tree}
For $D = \{1,\ldots,d\}$, $d \geq 2$, the \emph{Tensor Train} (TT)
tree~\cite{Oseledets2011} is the linear (chain) binary tree
\begin{equation*}
    \TDtt = \{D\} \cup \bigl\{\{j\} : 1 \leq j \leq d\bigr\}
           \cup \bigl\{\{1,\ldots,j\} : 2 \leq j \leq d-1\bigr\},
\end{equation*}
with $\Sons{\{1,\ldots,j\}} = \{\{1,\ldots,j-1\},\{j\}\}$
for $j \geq 2$. Here $\dep{\TDtt} = d - 1$.
The level partitions are:
\begin{equation*}
    \mathcal{P}_k(\TDtt) =
    \bigl\{\{1,\ldots,d-k\},\,\{d-k+1\},\,\ldots,\,\{d\}\bigr\},
    \quad 1 \leq k \leq d-1.
\end{equation*}
\end{example}

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\synttree[$\{1,2,3,4,5\}$[$\{1,2,3,4\}$[$\{1,2,3\}$[$\{1,2\}$[$\{1\}$][$\{2\}$]][$\{3\}$]][$\{4\}$]][$\{5\}$]]
\caption{Tensor Train tree over $D=\{1,2,3,4,5\}$: depth~$4$, linear
binary structure.}
\label{fig:TT_tree}
\end{figure}

\begin{remark}
\label{rem:TT_as_HT}
The TT format is a special case of the HT format: $\TDtt$ is a binary
tree with maximal depth $d-1$ and a linear (path) structure, whereas HT
trees are typically balanced with depth $\lceil \log_2 d \rceil$.
This is reflected in their computational cost: TT has storage
$O(dr^2 n)$ while balanced HT has $O(d r^3)$, with $r$ the maximal
rank and $n = \max_j \dim V_j$~\cite{HaKuehn2009, Oseledets2011}.
\end{remark}

\section{Graph-theoretic properties}
\label{sec:trees:graph}
%------------------------------------------------------------

We record here the combinatorial facts about dimension partition trees
that will be used in Chapters~\ref{chap:algTBT} and~\ref{chap:TB_manifold}.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:tree_cardinality}
Let $\TD$ be a dimension partition tree over $D = \{1,\ldots,d\}$.
Then:
\begin{enumerate}
\item[\textup{(i)}] $\card{\Leaves} = d$. The number of internal
    \textup{(}non-leaf\textup{)} vertices satisfies
    $\card{\TD \setminus \Leaves} \leq d - 1$.
\item[\textup{(ii)}] If $\TD$ is binary, then
    $\card{\TD} = 2d - 1$, with exactly $d - 1$ internal vertices.
\item[\textup{(iii)}] $\dep{\TD} \leq d - 1$, with equality if and
    only if $\TD = \TDtt$ (up to re-labelling).
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Part~(i): every leaf is a singleton $\{j\}$ for a unique $j \in D$,
so $\card{\Leaves} = d$. The bound on internal vertices follows since
each internal vertex contributes at least two children which are
eventually singletons. Part~(ii): in a binary tree with $d$ leaves,
the number of internal nodes is exactly $d - 1$, giving $2d-1$ total
vertices. Part~(iii): the depth is maximised when each step reduces
the largest set by exactly one element, giving the TT structure.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Recursive subtree structure]
\label{prop:subtree}
Let $\TD$ be a dimension partition tree over $D$ and
$\alpha \in \TD$ with $\card{\alpha} \geq 2$. Then the restriction
\begin{equation*}
    \TD|_\alpha \coloneqq \{\beta \in \TD : \beta \subseteq \alpha\}
\end{equation*}
is itself a dimension partition tree over $\alpha$ (with root $\alpha$).
\end{proposition}

This recursive structure is fundamental to the inductive proofs
in Chapter~\ref{chap:algTBT}: properties of tree-based tensor formats
are proved by induction on $\dep{\TD}$, reducing at each step to the
subtrees $\TD|_\beta$ for $\beta \in \Sons{D}$.

\section{The intersection representation}
\label{sec:trees:intersection}
%------------------------------------------------------------

The central structural theorem of the tree-based theory expresses the
set of tree-based tensors with fixed rank as an intersection of Tucker
manifolds — one at each level of the tree. This is the key result
connecting the tree structure to the geometry of Chapters~\ref{chap:TB_manifold}.

For each level partition $\PkT{k}{\TD}$, recall from
Section~\ref{sec:algtensor:tucker} that the identification
$\VD \cong \bigotimes_{\alpha \in \PkT{k}{\TD}} \Vbigalpha$ allows one
to define the Tucker set
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:tucker_level_k}
    \Tucker{\fr_k}{\VD, \PkT{k}{\TD}}
    \coloneqq
    \bigl\{\bv \in \VD :
        \dim \Umin{\alpha}{\bv} = r_\alpha
        \text{ for all } \alpha \in \PkT{k}{\TD}
    \bigr\},
\end{equation}
where $\fr_k = (r_\alpha)_{\alpha \in \PkT{k}{\TD}}$.

\begin{theorem}[Intersection representation, {\cite[Theorem~3.12]{FHN2023}}]
\label{thm:intersection_representation}
Let $\TD$ be a dimension partition tree over $D$ and
$\fr = (r_\alpha)_{\alpha \in \TD} \in \ADTD$. Then
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:intersection_representation}
    \FTv = \bigcap_{k=1}^{\dep{\TD}}
    \Tucker{\fr_k}{\VD, \PkT{k}{\TD}},
\end{equation}
where $\fr_k = (r_\alpha)_{\alpha \in \PkT{k}{\TD}}$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\textbf{($\subseteq$)} Let $\bv \in \FTv$, i.e., $\dim\Umin{\alpha}{\bv}
= r_\alpha$ for all $\alpha \in \TD$. For each level $k$ and each
$\alpha \in \PkT{k}{\TD}$, the rank $r_\alpha(\bv)$ equals $r_\alpha$
by definition, so $\bv \in \Tucker{\fr_k}{\VD, \PkT{k}{\TD}}$. Since
this holds for all levels, $\bv \in \bigcap_k
\Tucker{\fr_k}{\VD, \PkT{k}{\TD}}$.

\textbf{($\supseteq$)} Let $\bv \in \bigcap_{k=1}^{\dep{\TD}}
\Tucker{\fr_k}{\VD, \PkT{k}{\TD}}$, i.e., $\dim\Umin{\alpha}{\bv}
= r_\alpha$ for every $\alpha$ at every level of $\TD$. We must show
$\bv \in \FTv$, i.e., that the same holds for every $\alpha \in \TD$
(not just the level-$k$ vertices).

The vertices of $\TD$ at each level already cover all of $\TD$:
$\TD = \{D\} \cup \bigcup_{k=1}^{\dep{\TD}} \PkT{k}{\TD}$. (Every
non-root vertex appears in exactly one level, and the root $D$ has
$r_D$ fixed by the rank of $\bv$.)

It remains to verify the tree compatibility condition
$\Umin{\alpha}{\bv} \subset \bigotimes_{\beta \in \Sons{\alpha}}
\Umin{\beta}{\bv}$ for each internal vertex $\alpha \in \TD$.
By Proposition~\ref{prop:min_inclusion_partition} applied to the
partition $\mathcal{P}_\alpha = \Sons{\alpha}$ of $\alpha$:
\begin{equation*}
    \Umin{\alpha}{\bv}
    \subset {}_a\bigotimes_{\beta \in \Sons{\alpha}} \Umin{\beta}{\bv}.
\end{equation*}
This holds for every $\alpha \in \TD \setminus \Leaves[\TD]$ purely from
the definition of minimal subspaces and does not require any hypothesis
on the rank. Since $\dim\Umin{\alpha}{\bv} = r_\alpha$ for all
$\alpha \in \TD$ by assumption, we have $\bv \in \FTv$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
\label{rem:tucker_as_intersection}
For $\TD = \TDtuck$ (depth~1), the intersection
\eqref{eq:intersection_representation} reduces to a single factor:
$\FT{\fr}{\VD}{\TDtuck} = \Tucker{\fr_1}{\VD, \Leaves} =
\Tucker{\fr}{\VD}$, recovering the Tucker format.
\end{remark}

\begin{example}
\label{ex:intersection_d4}
For $D = \{1,2,3,4\}$ and the balanced HT tree
$\TDht$ of Example~\ref{ex:HT_tree} with
$\fr = (r_D, r_{\{1,2\}}, r_{\{3,4\}}, r_{\{1\}}, r_{\{2\}},
r_{\{3\}}, r_{\{4\}})$:
\begin{align*}
    \FT{\fr}{\VD}{\TDht}
    &= \Tucker{\fr_1}{\VD, \mathcal{P}_1}
     \cap \Tucker{\fr_2}{\VD, \mathcal{P}_2},
\end{align*}
where $\mathcal{P}_1 = \{\{1,2\},\{3,4\}\}$ and
$\mathcal{P}_2 = \Leaves$.
Concretely:
\begin{align*}
    \Tucker{\fr_1}{\VD, \mathcal{P}_1}
    &= \bigl\{\bv \in \VD :
        \dim \Umin{\{1,2\}}{\bv} = r_{\{1,2\}},\;
        \dim \Umin{\{3,4\}}{\bv} = r_{\{3,4\}}\bigr\}, \\
    \Tucker{\fr_2}{\VD, \mathcal{P}_2}
    &= \bigl\{\bv \in \VD :
        \dim \Umin{\{j\}}{\bv} = r_{\{j\}},\; j=1,2,3,4\bigr\}.
\end{align*}
The intersection captures tensors that are simultaneously Tucker
at the level of pairs $(\{1,2\},\{3,4\})$ and at the level of
singletons.
\end{example}

\begin{remark}[Degenerate case]
\label{rem:degenerate}
Not all intersections define distinct sets. If for some internal
vertex $\alpha \in \TD$ the rank $r_\alpha$ equals
$\prod_{\beta \in \Sons{\alpha}} r_\beta$, the rank constraint at
$\alpha$ is automatically satisfied whenever the constraints at
the children are. In such cases one of the Tucker factors in
\eqref{eq:intersection_representation} is redundant.
This situation is analysed in detail in~\cite[Theorem~3.12]{FHN2023}.
\end{remark}

\section{Concrete comparisons: Tucker, HT, and TT for $d=3,4,5$}
\label{sec:trees:comparison}
%------------------------------------------------------------

This section works through the three standard formats in detail for
small values of $d$, comparing storage costs, rank constraints, and
the inclusion relations between the corresponding tensor sets.
Throughout, $V_\alpha = \RR^n$ for all $\alpha$ (so $n_\alpha = n$)
and $r$ denotes a uniform rank bound $r_\alpha = r$ for all
$\alpha \in \TD \setminus \{D\}$.

\subsection*{The case $d=3$}

For $D = \{1,2,3\}$ there are exactly two possible tree structures:

\paragraph{Tucker tree ($\dep = 1$).}
The only tree of depth~1 is $\TDtuck = \{D, \{1\},\{2\},\{3\}\}$
with $\Sons{D} = \{\{1\},\{2\},\{3\}\}$. A Tucker tensor
$\bv \in \Tucker{(r,r,r)}{\RR^n \otimes \RR^n \otimes \RR^n}$ has the form
\begin{equation*}
    \bv = \sum_{i_1,i_2,i_3=1}^r C_{i_1 i_2 i_3}\,
    u^{(1)}_{i_1} \otimes u^{(2)}_{i_2} \otimes u^{(3)}_{i_3},
    \quad C \in \RR^{r \times r \times r}_*.
\end{equation*}
Storage: $3rn$ (basis vectors) $+ r^3$ (core) $= 3rn + r^3$.

\paragraph{TT tree ($\dep = 2$).}
The only binary tree of depth~2 is $\TDtt =
\{D, \{1,2\}, \{1\},\{2\},\{3\}\}$ with
$\Sons{D} = \{\{1,2\},\{3\}\}$ and $\Sons{\{1,2\}} = \{\{1\},\{2\}\}$.
A TT tensor $\bv \in \FT{(r,r,r,r)}{\RR^n \otimes \RR^n \otimes \RR^n}{\TDtt}$
has the form
\begin{equation*}
    \bv = \sum_{i_1,i_2,i_3=1}^{n}
    \sum_{\alpha_1, \alpha_2=1}^{r}
    G^{(1)}_{i_1, \alpha_1} G^{(2)}_{\alpha_1, i_2, \alpha_2}
    G^{(3)}_{\alpha_2, i_3}\,
    e_{i_1} \otimes e_{i_2} \otimes e_{i_3},
\end{equation*}
with \emph{TT cores} $G^{(1)} \in \RR^{n \times r}$,
$G^{(2)} \in \RR^{r \times n \times r}$,
$G^{(3)} \in \RR^{r \times n}$.
Storage: $rn + r^2 n + rn = 2rn + r^2 n$.

\paragraph{Inclusion and storage comparison.}
By Theorem~\ref{thm:intersection_representation}, every TT tensor is
also a Tucker tensor (with Tucker rank $\leq (r^2, r, r)$ in general),
but not conversely. The storage comparison:

\begin{center}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.3}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule
Format & Tree depth & Storage ($d=3$, uniform rank $r$) \\
\midrule
Tucker & 1 & $3rn + r^3$ \\
TT & 2 & $2rn + r^2 n$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

For $r \ll n$, Tucker storage is $O(rn)$ while TT storage is $O(r^2 n)$:
Tucker is cheaper for $d=3$ because the core is only $r^3$ (small
when $r$ is small), while TT pays $r^2 n$ for the middle core.

\subsection*{The case $d=4$}

For $D = \{1,2,3,4\}$ the three standard formats are:

\paragraph{Tucker tree ($\dep = 1$).}
$\Sons{D} = \{\{1\},\{2\},\{3\},\{4\}\}$.
Storage: $4rn + r^4$.

\paragraph{Balanced HT tree ($\dep = 2$).}
From Example~\ref{ex:HT_tree}:
$\Sons{D} = \{\{1,2\},\{3,4\}\}$, each split further into singletons.
An HT tensor has the form
\begin{equation*}
    \bv = \sum_{k=1}^{r_{12}} \sum_{l=1}^{r_{34}}
    C_{kl}\, \mathbf{u}^{(12)}_k \otimes \mathbf{u}^{(34)}_l,
\end{equation*}
where $\mathbf{u}^{(12)}_k \in V_1 \otimes V_2$ are themselves Tucker
tensors:
\begin{equation*}
    \mathbf{u}^{(12)}_k
    = \sum_{i_1,i_2=1}^{r}
    B^{(12)}_{k, i_1, i_2}\, u^{(1)}_{i_1} \otimes u^{(2)}_{i_2},
\end{equation*}
and similarly for $\mathbf{u}^{(34)}_l$.
With uniform rank $r$ at all levels:
storage $= 4rn + 2r^2 r + r^2 = 4rn + 2r^3 + r^2$.

\paragraph{TT tree ($\dep = 3$).}
$\TDtt$ for $d=4$: $\Sons{D} = \{\{1,2,3\},\{4\}\}$,
$\Sons{\{1,2,3\}} = \{\{1,2\},\{3\}\}$,
$\Sons{\{1,2\}} = \{\{1\},\{2\}\}$.
Storage: $rn + r^2 n + r^2 n + rn = 2rn \cdot 2 + 2r^2 n = 4rn + 2r^2 n$.

\paragraph{Storage comparison ($d=4$).}
\begin{center}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.3}
\begin{tabular}{@{}llll@{}}
\toprule
Format & Tree depth & Storage ($d=4$) & Leading term \\
\midrule
Tucker & 1 & $4rn + r^4$ & $r^4$ (exponential in $d$) \\
HT (balanced) & 2 & $4rn + 2r^3 + r^2$ & $r^3$ \\
TT & 3 & $4rn + 2r^2 n$ & $r^2 n$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

For $r \ll n$: Tucker $\gg$ HT $\gg$ TT in storage. The Tucker core
$r^4$ grows exponentially in $d$, while HT and TT have polynomial
storage in $r$.

\subsection*{The case $d=5$}

\begin{example}[Three trees for $d=5$]
\label{ex:three_trees_d5}
For $D = \{1,2,3,4,5\}$, the three standard formats correspond to:

\paragraph{Tucker ($\dep = 1$).}
$\Sons{D} = \{\{1\},\{2\},\{3\},\{4\},\{5\}\}$.
Storage: $5rn + r^5$.

\paragraph{Balanced HT ($\dep = 3$).}
One natural binary tree splits as:
$\Sons{D} = \{\{1,2,3\},\{4,5\}\}$,
$\Sons{\{1,2,3\}} = \{\{1,2\},\{3\}\}$,
$\Sons{\{1,2\}} = \{\{1\},\{2\}\}$,
$\Sons{\{4,5\}} = \{\{4\},\{5\}\}$.
Storage (uniform rank $r$): $5rn + 4r^3 + r^2$.

\paragraph{TT ($\dep = 4$).}
$\Sons{\{1,\ldots,j\}} = \{\{1,\ldots,j-1\},\{j\}\}$ for $j = 5,4,3,2$.
Storage: $2rn + 3r^2 n$.
\end{example}

\begin{remark}[The curse of dimensionality and its mitigation]
\label{rem:curse_dimensionality}
The fundamental issue is that for a full tensor $\bv \in
\bigotimes_{j=1}^d \RR^n$, storage requires $n^d$ numbers — exponential
in $d$. The three formats address this differently:
\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{Tucker}: storage $drn + r^d$. The factor $r^d$ is still
    exponential in $d$, so Tucker is practical only for small $d$
    (typically $d \leq 6$) or when $r = 1$ (separable tensors).
\item \emph{HT}: storage $O(dr^3)$. The core tensors at each internal
    node have size $r \times r \times r$, independent of $n$. For
    balanced binary trees with $d$ leaves and depth $\lceil\log_2 d\rceil$,
    the total cost is $O(d(rn + r^3))$.
\item \emph{TT}: storage $O(dr^2 n)$. The dependence on $n$ is linear
    and the dependence on $r$ is quadratic, but the cost is independent
    of $d$ up to a linear factor. TT is the most efficient for large $d$.
\end{itemize}
The choice of tree reflects the entanglement/correlation structure of
the problem: problems with local interactions (e.g., 1D quantum chains,
tridiagonal systems) are efficiently represented by TT; problems with
hierarchical correlations (e.g., multi-scale PDEs, wavelet bases) are
better suited to HT.
\end{remark}

\subsection*{Inclusion relations}

The intersection representation (Theorem~\ref{thm:intersection_representation})
implies the following inclusion chain for the same dimension $d$ and
rank bound $r$, comparing formats by number of constraints:

\begin{proposition}[Inclusion of tensor sets]
\label{prop:inclusion_formats}
Let $\TD$ and $\TD'$ be two dimension partition trees over $D$ with
$\dep{\TD} \leq \dep{\TD'}$. If every level partition of $\TD'$
refines the corresponding level partition of $\TD$, then
\begin{equation*}
    \FT{\fr'}{\VD}{\TD'} \subseteq \FT{\fr}{\VD}{\TD},
\end{equation*}
where $\fr'$ and $\fr$ are related by the rank bounds at the
corresponding nodes.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By Theorem~\ref{thm:intersection_representation}, $\FT{\fr'}{\VD}{\TD'}$
is an intersection over more levels than $\FT{\fr}{\VD}{\TD}$, hence
a subset.
\end{proof}

For fixed $d$ and uniform rank $r$, the inclusion chain is:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:inclusion_chain}
    \FT{\fr}{\VD}{\TDtt} \subseteq \FT{\fr}{\VD}{\TDht}
    \subseteq \Tucker{\fr}{\VD} = \FT{\fr}{\VD}{\TDtuck},
\end{equation}
since $\TDtt$ has the most levels (deepest tree) and $\TDtuck$ has
the fewest (shallowest). Each inclusion is in general strict: a Tucker
tensor need not satisfy the additional structural constraints of HT or TT.

\section{Historical notes}
\label{sec:trees:history}
%------------------------------------------------------------

Dimension partition trees formalise the hierarchical structure of
tensor representations that had appeared in various forms in the
literature. The HT format was introduced by Hackbusch and Kühn
in~\cite{HaKuehn2009} using a binary tree structure, with the specific
aim of reducing the exponential storage of full Tucker tensors to
$O(d r^3)$. The TT format, independently introduced by Vidal~\cite{Vidal2003}
in quantum information (under the name \emph{matrix product states}) and
by Oseledets~\cite{Oseledets2011} in numerical mathematics
(as \emph{TT decomposition}), corresponds to the linear chain binary tree.

The general notion of a dimension partition tree, unifying these formats
under a common combinatorial framework, was introduced in~\cite{FHN2021}.
The intersection representation (Theorem~\ref{thm:intersection_representation})
was established in~\cite{FHN2023} and is the key to the geometric theory
of Chapter~\ref{chap:TB_manifold}: it reduces the analysis of the
tree-based manifold $\FTv$ to the intersection of Tucker manifolds, each
of which has the structure established in Chapter~\ref{chap:tucker_manifold}.

The connection between dimension partition trees and quantum physics
is deeper than a mere formal analogy. In quantum information, a tensor
network is a collection of tensors connected by contraction along shared
indices, and the tree structure encodes the entanglement structure of a
quantum state. The area law for entanglement entropy in one-dimensional
quantum systems~\cite{Vidal2003} is the physical motivation for the TT
format; balanced binary trees encode area laws in higher-dimensional
systems. This connection is discussed further in
Chapter~\ref{chap:open}.
